# Foreign documents in France

France sets its own requirements for **foreign public documents** (birth/marriage
certificates, diplomas…) submitted to a consulate, préfecture, university or town hall.
The requirement is the same **whatever the country the document comes from** — that
country only provides the mechanism to authenticate its own paper (see, for a Nigerian
document, [ng/demarches/authenticate-documents](../../ng/demarches/authenticate-documents.md)).

France asks for two things:

## 1. Proof the document is genuine — apostille OR legalisation
- If the issuing country is a **Hague Apostille Convention** member, an **apostille**
  from that country is enough.
- If it is **not** a member, the document needs **consular legalisation** (authenticated
  in the origin country, then legalised by the French embassy/consulate there).
- Which applies depends on the **origin country's** status — check it, then follow that
  country's authenticate-documents guide for the exact chain.

## 2. A sworn French translation
Unless the receiving body explicitly accepts the original language, the document must be
translated into French by a **traducteur assermenté** — a translator enrolled on a
French Cour d'appel list. (The layout of the translation varies by translator; what
matters is only that the translator is sworn.)

## Practical notes
- **Order matters**: translating *before* the document is apostilled/legalised can mean
  redoing it — confirm the sequence the receiving body wants first.
- **Original + copy**: French authorities usually want the original plus a photocopy.
- Requirements vary by the specific authority (consulate, préfecture, university) —
  confirm with the one handling your file.

## Pièges connus
- Assuming a plain photocopy or an English document is accepted (it usually isn't).
- Using a non-sworn translator (a regular/agency translation is rejected).
- Getting the translation done before legalisation, then having to redo it.
