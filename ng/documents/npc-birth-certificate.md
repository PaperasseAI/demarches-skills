# NPC Certificate of Birth (Nigeria)

The **National Population Commission (NPC)** is the federal body that registers
births in Nigeria and issues the **Certificate of Birth** under the *Births, Deaths,
Etc. (Compulsory Registration) Act, Cap B9, Laws of the Federation of Nigeria, 2004*.
This is the birth document foreign authorities (French consulate, Campus France,
universities) expect — and it is what item "birth certificate from the National
Population Commission" on the Campus France checklist refers to.

## Which document — don't confuse the two
- **NPC Certificate of Birth** — the full civil-status certificate (fields below).
  This is the one to present abroad.
- **Attestation of Birth** — an interim/short slip (often issued by a hospital or at
  registration, sometimes for children under a certain age). It is **not** the
  Certificate of Birth and is frequently rejected abroad. If you only have an
  attestation, obtain the full NPC Certificate of Birth before a visa/study file.
- An **age declaration** (sworn affidavit at a court) is a fallback only for people
  with no registered birth — weaker than an NPC certificate; avoid relying on it for
  a consulate if a real certificate can be obtained.

## What it looks like — fields on the certificate
A current NPC Certificate of Birth ("ORIGINAL" marked top-right, green guilloché
security border, NPC seals in the corners) carries:
- **Registration Center**, **LGA** (Local Government Area), **State**
- the **registration date** ("registered on … at the registration center")
- **Certificate Number** — a state-prefixed alphanumeric (e.g. begins with the state
  code), repeated at the bottom next to a **QR code**
- **National Identification Number (NIN)** — the holder's 11-digit NIN (often shown
  partly masked)
- **Full Name** (surname, then given names), **Sex**, **Date of Birth**
- **LGA/State of Birth**, **Locality of Birth**
- **Full Name of Mother**, **Full Name of Father**
- **Name of Registrar**, issue **Date**, and the **NPC Chairman** signature line
- a **Tracking ID** (may read "N/A" on some issues)
- a falsification caution (falsifying or using a false certificate is an offence)

Older handwritten certificates and pre-QR issues exist and are valid, but the current
QR/NIN-bearing version is the most readily accepted and verifiable abroad.

## How to obtain or replace it
- Register/obtain at an **NPC office or LGA registration centre**; recent registrations
  are captured in the NPC's electronic system and produce the QR-coded certificate.
- For a **lost or damaged** certificate, request a **re-issue/reprint** from the NPC
  (bring identifying details — full name, date and place of birth, parents' names, and
  the NIN or original certificate number if you have it). Confirm the current procedure
  and any fee with the NPC or the issuing centre — processes and digitisation vary by
  state and change over time.
- Verify a certificate's authenticity via its **QR code / Certificate Number** where
  the issue supports it.

## Using it abroad (e.g. France)
A Nigerian civil-status document is not accepted abroad on its own. Two separate things
are needed:
- **Authenticate the Nigerian document** for use abroad (notary → MFA Abuja →
  destination embassy) — see
  [authenticate-documents](../demarches/authenticate-documents.md).
- **Meet the destination country's own requirement** for foreign documents. For France
  that is legalisation/apostille + a sworn French translation — see
  [France's foreign-document rules](../../fr/pieces/foreign-documents.md).

Confirm specifics with the **French Embassy in Abuja** / the consulate handling your
file — requirements vary and change.

## Pièges connus
- Presenting an **attestation of birth** where a **Certificate of Birth** is required.
- Bringing the certificate abroad **without legalisation + sworn French translation**.
- Name-order mismatches (surname vs given names) between the certificate, passport and
  academic documents — consulates flag inconsistencies; make sure they match.
