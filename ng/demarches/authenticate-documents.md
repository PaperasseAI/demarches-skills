# Authenticating Nigerian documents for use abroad

A Nigerian public document is not accepted by a foreign authority on its own — it must
first be **authenticated** so the destination country can trust it is genuine. This is
the Nigerian-side mechanism; it is the same chain **whatever the destination country**.
What the destination then requires (translation, its own legalisation, formats) is set
by that country — see its own "foreign documents" rules (for France:
[fr/pieces/foreign-documents](../../fr/pieces/foreign-documents.md)).

## The chain (typical order — confirm current steps with the destination's embassy)
1. **Get the genuine original** from the issuing body (e.g. the NPC Certificate of
   Birth, not an attestation — see
   [npc-birth-certificate](../documents/npc-birth-certificate.md)).
2. **Notary Public** — notarise/attest the document where required.
3. **Federal Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Abuja)** — authenticate the document. This is
   the core Nigerian-side step; documents are authenticated at the MFA in Abuja.
4. **The destination country's embassy in Nigeria** — legalise the MFA-authenticated
   document (e.g. the French Embassy in Abuja for a document going to France).
   Requirements and fees vary by embassy; check the specific one.

## Apostille vs consular legalisation
The Hague Apostille Convention lets member countries replace consular legalisation with
a single "apostille" stamp. **Nigeria is not, in current practice, an apostille country
for this purpose**, so Nigerian documents go through the **consular legalisation** chain
above. (Nigeria's status has occasionally been reported as changing — confirm with the
destination's embassy before you start, so you don't pay for the wrong process.)

## Practical notes
- **Start early**: the MFA + embassy steps are the usual bottleneck in a visa timeline.
- **Keep originals + copies**: destinations typically want the **original plus a
  photocopy**.
- **Translation is the destination's rule, not this step** — do it in the order the
  destination wants (often *after* legalisation); check their foreign-documents card.
- Use the **official bodies**; a paid "agent" cannot skip the MFA/embassy authentication
  that is what actually makes the document valid.

## Pièges connus
- Presenting an **attestation** instead of the full certificate (rejected at step 1).
- Assuming an **apostille** suffices (it generally doesn't for Nigeria).
- Leaving the MFA/embassy steps to the last weeks before travel.
